Risk Factors & Diagnostic Tests
During Pregnancy

Identify maternal, fetal, and obstetric risk factors. Master first, second, and third trimester screening and diagnostic tests — from ultrasound to amniocentesis, NST to BPP.

Maternal Risk Factors

👩 Age extremes
• <35 years: risk of chromosomal abnormalities, pregnancy-induced hypertension, gestational diabetes, preterm birth
• <18 years: increased risk of preeclampsia, preterm birth, low birth weight
• Women over 40 have higher risk of stillbirth and cesarean
📋 Medical conditions
• Chronic hypertension → preeclampsia, IUGR, abruption
• Diabetes (pre-gestational) → congenital anomalies, macrosomia
• Thyroid disorders, heart disease, renal disease, autoimmune disorders, thrombophilia
🍷 Lifestyle & social
• Smoking → IUGR, preterm birth, placental abruption
• Alcohol → fetal alcohol spectrum disorders
• Illicit drugs (cocaine, opioids) → abruption, withdrawal, growth restriction
• Poor nutrition, low socioeconomic status, limited prenatal care
🩺 Obstetric history
• Prior preterm birth, stillbirth, neonatal death
• Prior cesarean (risk of uterine rupture, placenta accreta)
• Grand multiparity (≥5) → increased hemorrhage, malpresentation
• History of preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, or macrosomia

Fetal & Obstetric Risk Factors

  • Multiple gestation: Increased risk of preterm labor, preeclampsia, IUGR, malpresentation, postpartum hemorrhage.
  • Polyhydramnios / Oligohydramnios: Associated with fetal anomalies, placental insufficiency, cord prolapse.
  • Placental abnormalities: Placenta previa (painless bleeding), placental abruption (painful bleeding, abruption), vasa previa.
  • Fetal growth abnormalities: Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) or macrosomia (>4000–4500g).
  • Fetal anomalies: Structural or chromosomal (detected by ultrasound or genetic testing).
  • Rh incompatibility: Rh-negative mother with Rh-positive fetus → isoimmunization, hemolytic disease of the newborn.
  • Infections (TORCH): Toxoplasmosis, rubella, CMV, herpes, others (syphilis, parvovirus B19, Zika).
⚠️ High‑risk pregnancy requires specialized care (maternal‑fetal medicine referral). Serial growth ultrasounds, NST/BPP, and timely delivery planning.

First Trimester Tests (0–13 weeks)

✅ Urine/Serum Pregnancy Test
Detects hCG. Urine qualitative (home), serum quantitative (β‑hCG) confirms pregnancy and monitors viability.
🩸 Complete Blood Count (CBC)
Detects anemia, platelet disorders, infection. Baseline for hemoglobin, hematocrit, WBC.
🩸 Blood Type & Rh, Antibody Screen
Identifies Rh‑negative mothers → need Rh immune globulin. Antibody screen detects preexisting isoimmunization.
🧬 Rubella, Hepatitis B, Syphilis, HIV
Mandatory screening. Rubella non‑immune → vaccinate postpartum. Treat STIs to prevent vertical transmission.
🔬 Urinalysis & Culture
Detects asymptomatic bacteriuria (increased pyelonephritis risk), proteinuria (pre‐eclampsia), glucosuria (gestational diabetes clue).
🔬 Pap Smear & STI Screening
If due; gonorrhea/chlamydia screening recommended for high‑risk women.
🖥️ Transvaginal/Transabdominal Ultrasound
Dating (crown‑rump length), confirm viability, detect multiple gestation. Nuchal translucency (NT) at 11–13⁺⁶ weeks – part of first trimester screening.
🧬 Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS)
10–13 weeks. Diagnostic test for chromosomal abnormalities (e.g., Down syndrome). Small risk of miscarriage (0.5–1%).
📌 First trimester combined screening (NT ultrasound + PAPP‑A, free β‑hCG) detects ~85% of Down syndrome cases.

Second Trimester Tests (14–27 weeks)

🧪 Maternal Serum AFP (MSAFP)
15–20 weeks. Elevated → neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly). Low → risk of Down syndrome and trisomy 18.
🔬 Quadruple Screen (Quad screen)
MSAFP + hCG + uE3 + inhibin A. Detects Down syndrome (75–80%), trisomy 18, neural tube defects.
🖥️ Fetal Anatomy Ultrasound (Level II)
18–22 weeks. Evaluates fetal structures (head, spine, heart, abdomen, extremities, placenta, amniotic fluid). Detects major anomalies.
🧬 Amniocentesis
15–20 weeks. Diagnostic for chromosomal disorders, neural tube defects (AFP in amniotic fluid). Miscarriage risk 0.1–0.3%.
🍬 Glucose Challenge Test (GCT) – 50g
24–28 weeks (or earlier if risk factors). 1‑hour plasma glucose ≥130–140 mg/dL → proceed to OGTT.
🍩 Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) – 100g, 3‑hr
Diagnostic for gestational diabetes. Fasting, 1h, 2h, 3h levels. Two or more elevated values → GDM.
✅ If MSAFP is elevated, order detailed ultrasound to rule out neural tube defects. Persistent elevation with no fetal anomaly suggests other causes (multiple gestation, incorrect dates).

Third Trimester Tests (28–40 weeks)

💊 50g Glucose Challenge (if not done in 2nd trimester)
Or repeat GDM screening in high‑risk women.
🦠 Group B Streptococcus (GBS) Screening
35–37 weeks. Rectovaginal culture. Positive → intrapartum penicillin prophylaxis to prevent neonatal sepsis.
🩸 Repeat CBC, Rh antibody titer
Check for anemia; Rh‑negative mother receives Rh immune globulin (RhoGAM) at 28 weeks and within 72h postpartum if infant Rh+.
🖥️ Biophysical Profile (BPP)
Ultrasound + NST. Five components: fetal breathing, movement, tone, amniotic fluid volume, NST (reactive). Score 8/10 or 10/10 is reassuring.
❤️ Non‑Stress Test (NST)
Monitors fetal heart rate reactivity. Reactive: ≥2 accelerations in 20 minutes. Non‑reactive → further evaluation (BPP or contraction stress test).
📈 Contraction Stress Test (CST)
Assesses fetal response to uterine contractions. Negative (no late decelerations) is reassuring. Positive (late decelerations) indicates uteroplacental insufficiency.
🩸 Doppler Velocimetry
Assesses umbilical artery blood flow. Abnormal (absent or reversed end‑diastolic flow) suggests placental insufficiency, often in IUGR or preeclampsia.
